Solving the 3x3 Rubik's Cube

So you want to learn how to solve the rubik's cube? Well, in this article I will teach you a simple method to do just that.

This tutorial may seem more complicated than others you have seen. While it is true that there are many tutorials that claim to solve the rubik's cube in 10 moves or less, they are all lying to you. There is no magic move or set of moves to solve the rubik's cube. Every person in history to have solved it either got lucky, or they followed a procedure similar to this one to reduce any scramble to simple, manageable steps.

This article walks through one of the simplest methods to solve a 3x3 rubik's cube known as the Beginner Method. There are many other methods out there, but they usually involve more complicated steps and have more things you need to learn and memorize. The most popular is known as CFOP. If you want a more advanced method, you should go check that out.

The Beginner Method is a layer-by-layer approach, which means that instead of solving one color at a time, you split the cube into three "layers" stacked on top of one another. Each layer consists of multiple phases:

  1. The First Layer:
    • The White Cross
    • The White Corners
  2. The Second Layer:
    • Second Layer Edges
  3. The Third Layer:
    • The Yellow Cross
      • Orientation of the Edges
      • Alignment of the Cross
    • The Yellow Corners
      • Positioning of the Corners
      • Orientation of the Corners

Solving the White Cross

First, you must solve the white cross. When you have finished this step, the cube will look like this (the gray pieces indicate that the piece could be any color, we don't care what the rest of the cube looks like right now):

Each edge piece with white on it will be between the white center and the center of the other color on that piece. (For example, the white-blue edge piece is touching the white center and the blue center).

Notice I said edge piece. This is important because while there are 54 stickers on the cube (6 sides with 9 stickers each), there are actually only 20 moving pieces.

The colors on each piece are stuck together, and can never change. This means that for a piece to be solved, all the colors on it must match the sides they're on.

There are three types of pieces:

  • Center pieces (6), which have only one color, and do not move because they are all attached to each other.
  • Edge pieces (12), which have two colors, and touch the center pieces and corner pieces. And
  • Corner pieces (8), which have three colors, and only touch edge pieces.

A common, and very natural, way to try to solve the rubik's cube is by solving one color or side at a time. But since pieces are inseparable, a better strategy is to solve the cube one layer at a time. The color most people start with is white, so in this tutorial the layer we will start with is white as well. You do not have to do it this way, but it makes it easier to understand the instructions.

To start solving the rubik's cube, we hold the cube with the white center on top, which will put the yellow center on the bottom since they are always opposite from each other.

We solve the white cross one piece at a time, so first, you need to find a white edge. To solve it, turn the cube until the piece is touching the yellow center (so the piece is on the bottom). Then turn the bottom until the side color of the piece matches one of the centers.

If that's impossible, look at the color on the other side of the piece (on the bottom of the cube), and hold that color center facing towards you (still with yellow on the bottom), and move your piece to one side of the cube (still on the bottom).

If you have the first case, it can be solved by just turning the side with the matching color twice so that the white side of the piece attaches to the white center. (fig c below)

The second case is a little more tricky. If your piece is on the left, you need to turn the left side once so that the side color matches (blue in the example), then turn the front until the white piece is on top, attached to the white center. (fig c) If its on the right, just do the same thing on the other side. However, if this is not your first cross piece, you will have just unsolved one of the others (red in the example), so you will then need to turn the left side (or right side) back the other way to fix it. The exact sequence of moves is L' F L (or R F' R'). To learn what these letters mean, visit this page: How To Read Move Notation. It will be very helpful to know later on.

(For the curious: the move for the first case is just F2)

To finish solving the cross, just repeat these steps for all four pieces.

In the first step (connecting the piece to the yellow center), you may end up unsolving a cross piece above the piece you found. In that case, you need to move your new piece to one side, then turn the front side back to fix your already solved cross piece. Then you can continue with the steps to solve your new piece.

You may also notice that a piece you find is already connected to its non-white center (for example, if your cube started already looking like fig a). If that is the case, you do not need to move it to the bottom first, you can just turn the front until the piece is on top.

To practice the cross, just solve the cross, and then mix up the cube again and solve the cross again. Keep mixing it up and solving it until you have a good grasp of the steps and the ideas. Once you are comfortable picking up a cube and solving the cross without too much trouble, then you should go on to the next section.

Completing the First Layer

The next step in solving the rubik's cube is to solve the white corners. The completed step will look like this:

You may have noticed in the figure that white is now on the bottom. For the rest of the solve, we want it that way. So flip over your cube now so that white is now on the bottom, and yellow is on top.

First, we need to find a white corner. That just means any corner with white on it.

Next, find the spot where it's supposed to go, then turn the top until the corner is directly above the spot where it is supposed to go.

Now rotate the cube so that the corner is in front, and the white sticker is on the side (as shown in fig d). Depending on which way the piece is rotated, it may need to be on the left or the right.

With white on the right, insert the corner like this: R U R'. This is called the right trigger. It will put in any corner on the right. If it's on the left use the left trigger: L' U' L.

Notice that "inserting" or solving the corners is very similar to edges that are flipped the wrong way, but now we are turning the top instead of the front.

There are two other cases that can happen as well. The corner piece may be already in the correct position, but oriented the wrong way, or it may be above its spot like it's supposed to, but the white sticker is facing up instead of to one side.

For the first case, once again hold the cube so that the white sticker is facing to the side, then do the right trigger if it's facing right (or the left trigger if it's facing left). Then move the corner back over to above its spot, and do the right (or left) trigger again to put it in the correct way.

For the second case, hold it on the right and do the right trigger. This will turn it into the first case, and you can solve it from there.

This skips the R' and R, and turns the two U moves into U2, creating the new move sequence R U2 R' U'

Once you do this, you may notice that you moved the corner down and then right back up. So you can actually just lift up the corner, and then turn the top twice instead of once, which will turn it into a normal corner, which you can solve using the right trigger.

As before with the cross edge pieces, repeat the steps for each corner until you have solved the first layer.

The Second Layer

The last thing to do before we get to the top layer is to solve the edges in the second layer. The completed step will look like this:

Of course, we first need to find a second layer edge. A second layer edge is any edge that does not have yellow on it (or whatever your top color is). Once you have found one, turn the top until it matches with one of the centers. This will always be possible, there are no bad cases for this.

Once you have your edge in place, make sure you are holding the cube so that the edge is on the front (as shown in fig f). This means that the spot it is supposed to go should also be on the front.

Now turn the top to move the edge piece away from the spot it's supposed to go. This sets up the algorithm to put the edge in its spot (This step is included as the first U or U' move in the algorithm).

In cubing, an algorithm is a sequence of moves. Any sequence of moves could be considered an algorithm, but we generally call something an algorithm when it does something useful, which usually means moving just a few pieces around to make sure we keep our current progress.

The edge insertion algorithm goes like this: U R U' R' y U' L' U L. I think of it like this. You first take out the corner from its spot, and in doing so, you are pairing up the corner and the edge to be put back in together. You then put the corner back in a different way so it changes the cube.

That is the essence of any good (useful) algorithm. Break some progress, then restore it in a different way. This will change something in the parts that you haven't solved yet, and move them around to solve them too.

The edge insertion algorithm above is for edges that need to go on the right. There is also the left-side version of the algorithm, which is exactly the same, but on the other side: U' L' U L y' U R U' R'.

That is all you need to know to solve the second layer. Once again, repeat it four times (once for each second layer edge) until you have solved the second layer of the rubik's cube!

Sometimes you may find a second layer edge that is in the second layer, but not in the correct spot, or maybe it is in the correct spot but flipped around. To solve either of these cases, just insert a pretend edge, which will take out the bad edge. Then you can put the edge back in correctly.

The Yellow Cross

The yellow cross consists of two steps: Orientation of the edges and Alignment of the yellow cross. This is what the yellow cross look like:

The first step is the orientation of the cross. That looks like this:

Notice that the side colors of the cross don't matter yet. All we are doing in this step is making sure the yellow side of all the edges are facing up.

There is one algorithm you need to learn for this step. It goes like this: F R U R' U' F'.

There are four cases that can show up, as shown below.

If you got the cross, you're already done! If you got one of the other cases, just do the algorithm and it will turn into the next case.

If you don't have any of the cases listed above (in other words, if you have three edges correct, or only one edge correct) that means that someone has taken your cube apart and put an edge back in incorrectly.

There is no way to fix this using only legal moves, so you will need to take your cube apart a little bit to take one of the bad edges out, so that you can put it back in correctly. Then you can continue solving it as normal.

Make sure to hold the cube in the orientation shown in the examples. Hold the L-shape with one yellow edge pointing toward you, and the other pointing to the right. When solving the line, hold the cube so that the edges point to the left and the right. (The dot doesn't matter becuase it is the same from any direction).

To practice the yellow cross, you can just continue doing the cross algorithm.

When practicing just the algorithm, don't worry about which case you have. Just do it a bunch of times until your fingers can do it without even thinking.

Once you have the algorithm in muscle memory, then you should practice how to do each case. That means that you should scramble the entire cube and practice every step, making sure you end up with the yellow cross every time.

Now it is time to align the yellow cross. Here is the algorithm you need to know for this step: R U R' U R U2 R'.

There are three cases that can come up during this step. Either the cross will already be aligned (hooray!) or there will be two cross pieces next to each other that need to switch (like the L-shape case in the last step), or finally there could be two across from each other that need to switch (like the line case in the last step). That means that there will always be at least two edges in the correct position.

If you do not have two edges correct right now, turn the top layer until you do. This is always possible. There cannot be less than two correct.

Similar to the previous step, you will hold the cube in a specific orientation to solve each case. However, instead of thinking about the solved pieces, now you need to think about the unsolved pieces. In other words, if you have two unsolved edges next to each other (L-shape case), hold one pointing towards you and the other pointing to the right. For the line case, hold the unsolved edges on the left and right.

Once you are holding the cube the correct way, execute the algorithm to solve each case. The algorithm will turn the line case into the L-shape, and then doing the algorithm again will align the entire yellow cross.

After doing the algorithm to solve the L-shape, you will not have any edges aligned to the correct centers, but you just need turn the top until the cross is solved.

You now have the entire yellow cross solved! There are now only the yellow corners left to solve! To see me explain the alignment of the yellow cross, watch the video below.

First, practice the algorithm until you can do it with your eyes closed.

Then you should scramble the cube a few times and practice everything you have learned up to this point. Make sure to practice each case!

The Yellow Corners

You are on the last step! The cube is so close to being solved! You can do it!

Obviously, after this step the cube will be solved, but for consistency's sake, this is what the cube will look like after you have finished this step ;)

To start, we first put all of the corners in the right spot, then we will orient them correctly. This step is confusing to a lot of people, but it just turns that moving corners around is easier if we don't worry about their orientation, and it is also not too hard to turn corners while keeping them in the same position.

To put the corners in the right spot, first look for any that are already correct. You are looking for a corner that has the same three colors on it as the edges around it, even though it may not match the colors next to it. Example below.

There are three possibilities: either they are all correct already, or there is only one correct, or no corners are in the correct positions. You can never have only two. If you see two correct corners, that means that the other two must be correct as well.

If you somehow have two corners in the correct positions, and two in the incorrect positions, that means that the cube has been taken apart and two corners were switched. The only way to fix that is to physically take the two bad corners out and put them back in the correct positions.

If you find one correct corner, hold the cube so that the correct corner is in the front right position, and execute this algorithm: L' U R U' L U R' U'. If none are correct, hold it in any orientation and do the same algorithm.

You may do the algorithm and then discover that you still only have one corner, or you still do not have any. In that case, just do it again. After doing the algorithm at most twice, you are guaranteed to get the next case.

Now practice your yellow corners!

As before, practice the algorithm until you have it down, then scramble the cube and go back to the beginning to make sure you don't forget what you have learned.

At this point, every single piece on your cube will be in the correct position, and you will only have a few corners that need to be turned. It may be tempting to just forcefully turn them to the correct orientation, but that is not allowed in cubing. If you are just solving it so that it is no longer sitting mixed up on your shelf, you may feel justified in just turning them, but I promise you will not have the same satisfaction you would if you did it correctly all the way through.

That being said, the last step is not that hard anyway.

The algorithm you need to learn to solve twisted corners is just this: R' D' R D. That's it. Just hold a twisted yellow corner in the front right position and do that algorithm.

However, just doing that once will not solve anything. It will look like you have just messed up the cube. That's because, to turn a corner, you need to do the algorithm twice. Doing it twice takes the corner out of its spot, turns it, and puts it back in (so it will be twisted from its original orientation).

The rest of the cube will still look scrambled, but that's okay. All we care about right now is if the corner is solved. Once you have solve one, turn the top until a different twisted corner is in the front right position. (Important: do not turn the cube! Only turn the top!)

If your cube is not solved once you have solved each twisted corner, that means that someone has twisted one and only one corner on your cube, which is not possible to solve using only legal moves.

To fix it, keep doing the corner twist algorithm until the bottom of the cube is solved. This will make your corner unsolved again, so now you will have to twist it manually, which may involve taking the corner piece out and put it back in correctly.

To finish solving the entire rubik's cube, just repeat that step for each corner. Once you have done all of them, your cube will be solved!

Except that the top won't be aligned since you turned it to switch out the corners. So the very last step to solve the rubik's cube is just to turn the top face until your cube has the same colors on every side.
